The Science Behind InteroHR

InteroHR is inspired by research on interoception — the brain’s ability to sense signals from inside the body, such as heartbeats, breathing, and internal arousal.

Listening to the body

Every moment, the brain receives signals from inside the body — heartbeats, breathing rhythms, muscle tension, and subtle shifts in internal state. The ability to perceive these signals is called interoception.

Interoception is sometimes described as the brain’s internal sensing system. It helps us detect whether we are calm or stressed, energized or fatigued, focused or overwhelmed. These signals shape how we feel, how we interpret emotion, and how we respond to challenge.

Most of this sensing happens automatically. But research suggests that people differ in how accurately they perceive their own internal signals.

How interoception is studied

Scientists often study interoception using tasks that ask participants to estimate their own heartbeats without feeling their pulse or using a device. After a short interval, the participant reports how many beats they perceived, and that estimate is compared with the measured heart rate.

These experiments allow researchers to quantify interoceptive accuracy — how closely a person’s perception matches the physiological signal. Some people show strong alignment. Others show much less.

Why it matters

Interoception appears to play a role in emotional awareness, stress regulation, and decision making under uncertainty. In daily life, internal signals can shape whether we interpret a situation as manageable, threatening, energizing, or exhausting.

A body that feels legible from the inside may support clearer self-regulation. A body that feels noisy, vague, or misleading can make interpretation harder. The science is still evolving, but the basic question is a fascinating one: how much do internal signals help guide thought and behavior?

Instead of only asking what sensors detect, interoception asks a quieter question: what does the body feel like from the inside?

Can interoception improve?

Researchers are still exploring this. Some studies suggest that repeated attention to internal signals, especially when paired with structured feedback, may improve interoceptive accuracy in certain contexts.

At the same time, the field is nuanced. Different studies use different measurement methods, and the relationship between interoception, performance, emotion, and regulation is not always simple.

InteroHR takes a practical stance: rather than treating interoception as a fixed trait, it treats it as something worth observing, practicing, and understanding over time.

How InteroHR fits in

InteroHR is designed as a simple tool for exploring heart-rate awareness. The app invites users to estimate their heart rate under different conditions, compare perception with measured data, and observe how accuracy and regulation shift over time.

The goal is not perfection. The goal is awareness.

With repeated observation, the relationship between physiology and perception may become easier to recognize.

A note on interpretation

Interoception remains an active area of research, and scientists continue to debate the best ways to measure and interpret it.

InteroHR is not a medical tool and does not diagnose or treat any condition. It is a structured way to observe heart-rate perception and reflect on patterns over time.

Think of it as a personal instrument for exploring the signals of your own body.

A final thought

Modern technology measures our bodies constantly — steps, sleep, heart rate, activity.

Interoception turns the lens inward.

Instead of relying only on external sensors, it asks how accurately we can sense our own internal state. InteroHR is simply a tool to help explore that question.
